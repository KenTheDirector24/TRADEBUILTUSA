Impact Drivers

How impact drivers work, where they earn their keep on an HVAC/R job, and how to pick the right bits and settings.

What It Is

An impact driver is a compact, high-torque power tool built to drive screws and small fasteners fast, without stalling out or twisting out of your hand. It looks like a drill's smaller cousin, but the two tools solve different problems — a drill spins smoothly for boring holes and driving light fasteners; an impact driver adds concussive rotational force so it can drive long screws into dense material without you having to lean into it.

On an HVAC/R crew it's one of the most-used tools in the bag, right alongside a drill/driver and a set of nut drivers.

How It Works

Inside the housing, an impact driver pairs a motor with a spring-loaded hammer-and-anvil mechanism instead of the clutch you'd find on a drill/driver. As long as a screw is going in easily, the tool spins like any other driver. The moment it hits resistance — a stubborn fastener, dense material, a screw seating home — the hammer disengages, spins up, and slams into the anvil dozens of times per second. Each hit delivers a short burst of rotational force instead of steady twisting torque, which is how a tool this size can drive a long screw without stalling the motor or wrenching itself out of your wrist.

That impact action is also why the bit holder is a quick-release hex collet rather than a keyed or keyless chuck. Bits snap in and out one-handed, and the hex shank locks solidly enough to survive the hammering without slipping. Most impact drivers add a variable-speed trigger and a torque or speed dial on the body, so you can back the power down for delicate work and open it back up for lag bolts and structural screws.

Where It Earns Its Keep on an HVAC/R Job

On a service truck or install crew, the impact driver is what holds the job together — literally. It's the tool reaching for whenever sheet metal, brackets, or fasteners are involved:

  • Hanging and securing ductwork — strap hangers, trapeze supports, and sheet-metal screws through seams and collars
  • Mounting condensing units to pads, wall brackets, or roof curbs
  • Setting air handlers and furnaces on platforms, and fastening return and supply plenums
  • Running unistrut and framing for line sets, disconnects, and electrical whips
  • Fastening access panels, filter racks, and control box covers
  • Backing out old screws on equipment being replaced or serviced

It won't replace a drill for boring holes or a nut driver for low-torque precision work, but for driving a screw into metal, wood, or masonry fast and without babying it, it's the first tool most techs grab.

Picking the Right Bits and Settings

Standard driver bits aren't built for this tool — the hammering action will shear a cheap bit in a handful of uses. Impact-rated bits are hardened and slightly more flexible so they absorb the shock instead of snapping. Match the tip to the fastener: Phillips #2 for most sheet-metal screws, square-drive or Torx for cabinet and equipment panel screws, and a nut driver bit for the hex-head self-tappers common on ductwork and condensing units.

Speed and torque settings matter as much as the bit. Thin-gauge sheet metal and light brackets call for a low-torque setting — full power will strip the screw head or punch straight through the metal before you can react. Save the high-torque settings for lag screws, structural framing, and anything driving into wood or masonry.

Technique Tip

Let the tool do the work. Pulse the trigger instead of holding it wide open, especially on sheet metal — it gives you control over exactly when the hammer mechanism kicks in, so you can stop the instant the screw seats instead of overdriving it.
